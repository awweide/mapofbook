**Summary**

Anna Karenina, the beautiful and passionate wife of the high‑ranking Petersburg official Alexey Alexandrovitch Karenin, falls in love with the dashing cavalry officer Count Vronsky. Trapped in a loveless marriage and constrained by the rigid social codes of 1870s Russian aristocracy, Anna abandons her husband and her young son Seryozha to live openly with Vronsky. Initially ecstatic, Anna grows increasingly jealous and isolated as society shuns her and Vronsky’s ardor cools. In parallel, Konstantin Levin, a socially awkward landowner, marries Princess Kitty Shtcherbatskaya after surviving romantic disappointment. Levin struggles with faith, modernity, and the meaning of life, eventually finding spiritual peace. Anna, consumed by paranoia and despair, commits suicide by throwing herself under a train. Vronsky, devastated, volunteers for the war in Servia. Karenin raises Seryozha and Anna’s infant daughter. Levin, having discovered faith through lived experience rather than doctrine, finds joy in his family and daily labor.

---

**Author**

**Leo Tolstoy** (1828–1910) was a Russian novelist, philosopher, and moral thinker, universally regarded as one of the greatest writers in world literature. Born into an old aristocratic family at Yasnaya Polyana, he served as an artillery officer in the Crimean War before embarking on a literary career. His early masterpieces, *War and Peace* (1869) and *Anna Karenina* (1877), established his reputation for unparalleled psychological realism, epic scope, and deep moral seriousness. After completing *Anna Karenina*, Tolstoy underwent a profound spiritual crisis that led him to reject organized religion, private property, and his own earlier works in favor of a radical Christian anarchism. His later writings, including *The Death of Ivan Ilyich* (1886) and *Resurrection* (1899), reflect these beliefs. Tolstoy’s influence on twentieth‑century literature, from James Joyce to Virginia Woolf to Thomas Mann, is immeasurable.

---

**Importance**

*Anna Karenina* is widely considered one of the greatest novels ever written. It is the apotheosis of literary realism—a novel that balances intimate psychological portraiture with sweeping social critique, following multiple characters with symphonic coherence. Its opening sentence (“Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way”) has become proverbial. The novel has inspired countless adaptations, including ballets, operas, films, and television series, and its central character remains one of literature’s most complex, sympathetic, and tragic heroines.

---

**Themes**

- Adultery, jealousy, and the destructive power of passion
- The hypocritical moral codes of aristocratic society
- The tension between individual desire and social obligation
- Family happiness (the Oblonskys, the Levins) versus family destruction (the Karenins)
- Faith, doubt, and the search for life’s meaning
- The contrast between city (artificial, corrupt) and country (authentic, life‑giving)
- Modernity, railroads, industrialization, and their discontents
- Forgiveness, both Christian and human
- Death and the fear of death

---

**Message**

Tolstoy presents no simple moral lesson. Anna is not merely punished for adultery—she is destroyed by a society that tolerates the same infidelity in men (Stiva, Vronsky) but condemns women who transgress. Karenin’s initial Christian forgiveness is portrayed as admirable, yet it fails to save Anna or restore their marriage. Levin’s spiritual journey, culminating not in a creed but in a lived sense of goodness, suggests that meaning comes not from abstract philosophy but from loving others and working the land. The novel ultimately affirms that happiness lies not in romantic passion (which burns out) but in family, labor, and an acceptance of life’s mystery. Yet Anna’s tragedy remains fully tragic: she is not a villain but a woman who wanted to live fully, and was crushed for it.

### Motifs & Concepts

**The Train**  
*Representations:* train, railway, railroad  
*Explanation:* The train is a recurring motif of death, fate, and industrialization. Anna first meets Vronsky at a station where a guard is crushed. Her suicide is by train. The train represents the inexorable, mechanical force of modern life.  
*See also:* Anna, The Peasant Dream  
*Appearances:* 1.17–18, 1.29–30, 7.27–31

**The Peasant Dream**  
*Representations:* peasant dream, the little peasant, the nightmare  
*Explanation:* A recurring nightmare Anna has: a dirty little peasant rummaging in a sack, muttering French words. It symbolizes her guilt, fear of death, and the inescapable consequences of her actions. She recalls it before her suicide.  
*See also:* Anna  
*Appearances:* 4.4, 7.29

**The Red Bag**  
*Representations:* red bag, little red bag  
*Explanation:* Anna’s small red handbag. She drops it as she throws herself under the train. It symbolizes the domestic, feminine world she leaves behind.  
*See also:* Anna  
*Appearances:* 7.31

**The Candle**  
*Representations:* candle  
*Explanation:* At the moment of Anna’s death, the light of the candle by which she had been reading “flared up more brightly than ever, lighted up for her all that had been in darkness, flickered, began to grow dim, and was quenched forever.” It symbolizes her life, her hope, and her extinction.  
*See also:* Anna  
*Appearances:* 7.31

**The Horse Race (Steeplechase)**  
*Representations:* race, steeplechase, the races  
*Explanation:* A key event in Part 2. Vronsky rides Frou‑Frou and falls because of his own mistake, killing the mare. Anna’s public distress during the race leads her to confess her love to Karenin.

*Representations:* ball, the Moscow ball

*Representations:* divorce, get a divorce  
*Explanation:* Anna seeks a divorce from Karenin so she can marry Vronsky. Karenin refuses, then later agrees, then finally refuses again under Lidia Ivanovna’s influence. The divorce is never granted, leaving Anna’s children illegitimate.

### Motifs & Concepts – Additional

**The English Governess (Miss Hoole)**  
*Representations:* Miss Hoole, the English governess  
*Explanation:* The Oblonskys’ English governess. She appears briefly in the opening chapters. She represents the foreign influence in Russian aristocratic households.  
*See also:* Dolly, Stiva  
*Appearances:* 1.1, 1.20

**The French Governess (Mlle. Roland)**  
*Representations:* Mlle. Roland, the French governess  
*Explanation:* The governess with whom Stiva had an affair. She is the cause of the opening crisis. She is not directly seen in the novel.  
*See also:* Stiva, Dolly  
*Appearances:* 1.1, 1.2

**The Icon (Holy Picture)**  
*Representations:* holy picture, icon  
*Explanation:* At Levin and Kitty’s wedding, the priest blesses them with an icon. It represents the religious framework that Levin struggles with but ultimately accepts.  
*See also:* Levin, Kitty  
*Appearances:* 5.2

**The Chalk Code**  
*Representations:* chalk, writing on the table  
*Explanation:* At the dinner party, Levin and Kitty communicate by writing initial letters on the table with chalk. He asks if her refusal meant “never” or “then.” She replies. They become engaged.  
*See also:* Levin, Kitty  
*Appearances:* 4.11

**The Candle (Levin’s prayer)**  
*Representations:* candle, light  
*Explanation:* During Kitty’s labor, Levin prays desperately though he is an unbeliever. The candle imagery parallels Anna’s death scene. For Levin, the light is not extinguished but leads to faith.  
*See also:* Levin, Anna  
*Appearances:* 7.13–15

**The Railway Station**  
*Representations:* station, railway station, platform  
*Explanation:* Key locations: the Moscow station where Anna first meets Vronsky and a guard is killed; the Petersburg station; the small station where Anna commits suicide. Stations are liminal spaces where fate intervenes.  
*See also:* Anna, The Train  
*Appearances:* 1.17–18, 1.29–30, 7.27–31

**The Guard’s Death**  
*Representations:* guard crushed, the man killed by the train  
*Explanation:* As Anna arrives in Moscow, a railway guard is crushed by the train. She calls it an “omen of evil.” Vronsky gives 200 roubles to the widow. The event foreshadows Anna’s own death.  
*See also:* Anna, Vronsky, The Train  
*Appearances:* 1.18

**The Sewing Table**  
*Representations:* sewing table, writing‑table  
*Explanation:* In Part 2, Ch 26? Actually, the letters are found in Anna’s sewing table. In the script, Part 2, Ch 26? No – that’s the horse race. The discovery happens in Part 7? ## Major Storylines

| Title | Description | Appearances |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Anna and Vronsky’s Affair** | Anna, trapped in a loveless marriage, falls in love with the dashing officer Vronsky. Their affair leads to her social ruin, jealousy, and eventual suicide. This is the novel’s central tragic arc. | 1.18–23, 1.29–31, 2.4–11, 2.22–23, 2.28, 3.16–19, 3.23–25, 4.1–4, 4.17–23, 5.3–7, 5.16–21, 5.30–33, 6.12–22, 6.24–32, 7.23–31 |
| **Levin and Kitty’s Romance** | Levin proposes to Kitty and is refused. After Kitty’s humiliation by Vronsky and her illness abroad, they reconcile and marry. Their marriage, though difficult at first, becomes a source of happiness and stability. | 1.6, 1.9–15, 1.24, 2.1–3, 2.14–15, 2.30–35, 4.9–16, 5.1–2, 5.8–10, 5.22–29, 6.1–11, 7.1–16, 8.6–19 |
| **Stiva and Dolly’s Marriage** | Stiva’s infidelity with the French governess throws the Oblonsky household into chaos. Anna reconciles them, but Dolly continues to suffer from Stiva’s carelessness, financial mismanagement, and emotional neglect. | 1.1–4, 1.19–20, 1.28, 2.2–3, 3.7–8, 4.7–10, 5.22, 5.30–33, 6.12–17, 6.23 |
| **Karenin’s Forgiveness** | After Anna’s near‑death in childbirth, Karenin forgives her and Vronsky, experiencing a spiritual peace. Later, under Countess Lidia Ivanovna’s influence, he refuses divorce, hardening his heart. | 4.18–19, 4.22–23, 5.16–18, 7.17–22 |
| **Levin’s Spiritual Crisis** | Tormented by thoughts of death and the meaninglessness of life, Levin considers suicide. He reads philosophy but finds no answer. A peasant’s simple words (“to live for God, for the soul”) trigger a sudden faith, though he remains unable to articulate it. | 3.1–3, 3.12, 3.26–28, 5.8, 5.14–15, 8.6–19 |
| **Levin’s Agricultural Reforms** | Levin tries to modernize his farming and create a cooperative partnership with his peasants. The peasants resist, distrusting his motives. The failure of his scheme illustrates the gap between noble idealism and peasant reality. | 2.12–13, 3.26–29, 5.9–10, 6.1 |
| **The Horse Race** | Vronsky rides his mare Frou‑Frou in a steeplechase. He makes a fatal mistake; the horse falls and breaks her back. Anna’s public distress during the race leads her to confess her love to Karenin. | 2.24–26, 2.28 |
| **Anna and Her Son Seryozha** | Anna’s separation from her son Seryozha is one of her deepest pains. She secretly visits him on his birthday, an intensely emotional scene. After the divorce, Seryozha is raised to forget her. | 1.32, 4.5, 5.16–18, 7.19 |
| **Kitty’s Illness and Recovery Abroad** | After Vronsky’s rejection, Kitty falls into a physical and spiritual illness. The Shtcherbatskys go to a German spa, where she meets Varenka and Madame Stahl. Her father’s skepticism restores her. | 2.1–3, 2.30–35 |
| **Vronsky’s Attempted Suicide** | After Anna’s confession and Karenin’s forgiveness, Vronsky feels humiliated and worthless. He shoots himself in the chest but survives. The act reveals his despair and his sense of honor. | 4.17 |
| **The Slavonic Question and Servian War** | In Part 8, Russian society becomes obsessed with helping the Slavs against the Turks. Vronsky volunteers for the war, seeking death. Levin opposes war as murder. Sergey Ivanovitch champions the cause. | 8.1–5, 8.15–16 |
| **Nikolay Levin’s Death** | Levin’s consumptive brother Nikolay is dying. Levin and Kitty nurse him. His death forces Levin to confront the meaning of life and death. Kitty learns she is pregnant during this episode. | 1.8, 1.24–25, 3.31–32, 5.11–15 |
| **Anna’s Social Ostracism** | After leaving Karenin, Anna is cut by society. At the opera, Madame Kartasova publicly insults her. She is unable to visit friends or attend events. This isolation fuels her jealousy and despair. | 5.20–21, 6.12–17, 6.22, 7.23–24 |
| **Levin’s Jealousy of Veslovsky** | Vassenka Veslovsky visits the Levins and flirts with Kitty. Levin becomes insanely jealous and forces Veslovsky to leave. The incident shows Levin’s insecurity and Kitty’s devotion. | 5.25–29, 6.6–11 |
| **The Train as Death Motif** | Trains bookend the novel: a guard is crushed when Anna first meets Vronsky (an omen); Anna commits suicide by throwing herself under a train. The train represents industrial modernity, fate, and inescapable destruction. | 1.18, 1.29–30, 7.27–31 |
| **The Peasant Dream** | Anna has a recurring nightmare of a dirty little peasant rummaging in a sack, muttering French words. The dream terrifies her and returns before her suicide, symbolizing guilt and death. | 4.4, 7.29 |
| **Dolly’s Visit to Anna** | Dolly visits Anna and Vronsky at Vozdvizhenskoe. She sees Anna’s luxury and hears her fears. The visit deepens Dolly’s understanding of Anna’s position and her own marriage. | 5.30–33, 6.12–17 |
| **Sergey Ivanovitch and Varenka** | Sergey Ivanovitch considers proposing to Varenka during a mushroom‑picking walk. He hesitates, asks a trivial question instead, and the moment passes. He remains faithful to the memory of his dead love. | 5.23–24, 6.1–5 |
| **Levin’s Mowing with Peasants** | Levin joins his peasants in mowing, experiencing the bliss of physical labor. He envies their simple life and considers renouncing his own. This episode marks a turning point in his search for meaning. | 3.4–6 |
| **Karenin’s Political Career** | Karenin is a high‑ranking official. He works on a commission, writes reports, and is passed over for promotion. His career contrasts with his domestic misery and represents the hollow public world. | 1.5, 2.27, 4.5–6, 4.7, 7.17–18 |
